# Owner Conversation Guide

**Use:** 3–5 conversations with restaurant owners *who are not current customers*.
**Goal:** test whether the 8-goal catalog matches how owners actually think and talk — without leading them.
**Format:** 30 min. Phone, video, or coffee. Open-ended.

---

## Who to talk to

**Yes:**
- Independent restaurant operators (1–5 locations)
- $300–$3,000/month in marketing budget (or no budget but recognize they should)
- Have been operating ≥6 months (long enough to have marketing pain)
- From your network, friend-of-friend, cold outreach, industry events — anyone who fits the profile

**No (for this round):**
- Current Apnosh customers (their context is contaminated by the relationship)
- Enterprise / 20+ location operators (different customer)
- Pre-launch restaurants (haven't experienced the pain yet)
- Family or close friends who'll tell you what you want to hear

**Aim for variety:** different concept types (one neighborhood casual, one fine dining, one cafe, one mobile/food truck if possible). Single conversation = anecdote. Five conversations = pattern.

---

## How to open

Don't pitch. Don't even mention Apnosh. Open like this:

> "I'm doing some research on how restaurant owners actually handle marketing — what's working, what's a headache, what's a waste of time. Mind if I ask you some open-ended questions for 30 minutes? I'm not selling anything; I just want to understand."

If they ask "what's this for" — "I run a marketing agency for restaurants and I'm trying to make sure I'm building the right thing. Your honest answers help me more than your polite ones."

That's it. They'll talk.

---

## The five questions, in order

### Question 1 — open exploration

> **"Walk me through last week. Every marketing-ish thing you did or thought about. Which one took the most time?"**

This is the warm-up. They'll list activities. Listen for:
- What they consider "marketing" (some include hosting events; some only include social posts)
- Which felt like work vs. which felt productive
- The implicit goals embedded in what they did

**Don't interrupt.** Let them ramble for 5+ minutes.

### Question 2 — signal vs. noise

> **"Which one of those things made you feel like you actually got somewhere — like, you saw a real result? Which felt like it didn't matter?"**

Now you're testing what they perceive as effective. The "didn't matter" answers are gold — that's where they've burned trust on vendors.

### Question 3 — the pain

> **"If you could wake up tomorrow and hand off ONE marketing task forever, never deal with it again — what would it be?"**

This identifies the most-painful job-to-be-done. It's also a check on the goal catalog: if the answer is "responding to reviews," that maps to "better online reputation." If the answer is "thinking about what to post," that's content/social and maps to multiple goals.

### Question 4 — vendor experience

> **"Have you ever paid for marketing tools or services? What worked? What was a waste of money?"**

This validates (or not) the trust moat. If they have war stories — "I paid this guy $2k/month for 6 months and got nothing" — you've validated the decline-to-sell positioning. If they have no war stories, the moat might be less pressing for them.

Listen for vendor names. Specific names = real experience. Vague "some marketing companies" = secondhand.

### Question 5 — the goal in their language

> **"When you think about why you got into this restaurant business, what does success look like in 12 months?"**

This is where you hear the goal in their words. Don't interrupt. Don't translate.

After they answer, the follow-up:

> **"What would have to be true about marketing for that to happen?"**

Now they're translating success → marketing levers. Listen for which of the 8 goals they describe in their own words.

---

## What to listen for

Make a tally as they talk. After the conversation, mark each of the 8 goals:

| Goal | Mentioned unprompted? | In their words? |
|---|---|---|
| More foot traffic | Y/N | "more customers walking in" / "more first-time visitors" / etc. |
| Regulars come back more often | Y/N | "regulars" / "repeat" / "loyalty" / etc. |
| More online orders | Y/N | "delivery" / "online orders" / "ordering" / etc. |
| More reservations | Y/N | "bookings" / "reservations" / etc. |
| Better online reputation | Y/N | "reviews" / "rating" / "reputation" / etc. |
| Be known as the spot for ___ | Y/N | "known for" / "reputation" / "the spot" / etc. |
| Fill slow times | Y/N | "slow nights" / "lunch is dead" / etc. |
| Grow catering / private events | Y/N | "catering" / "events" / "parties" / etc. |

Also mark anything they bring up that **isn't** in the catalog. That might be a missing goal or a cousin of an existing one.

Also note words they use that we should adopt. "Foot traffic" might be "walk-ins" in their head. "Reputation" might be "what people say about us."

---

## What NOT to do

- **Don't pitch.** Even at the end. Especially at the end.
- **Don't describe the goal catalog.** You're testing whether goals emerge naturally; if you list them first, you've biased the data.
- **Don't ask "would you pay for this?"** Useless question. Everyone says yes in conversation; behavior tells the truth.
- **Don't argue with their description of marketing.** They might say something inaccurate — let it stand. You're learning their model, not correcting it.
- **Don't take notes during** if it makes them self-conscious. Notes from memory + voice memo immediately after work fine for 30-min conversations.
- **Don't try to recover an awkward question.** If you ask something and it lands wrong, just say "fair, scratch that — let me ask differently" and move on.

---

## After the conversations

After all 3–5, write a 1-page synthesis:

### Section 1: Goals that emerged unprompted, ranked by frequency

E.g., "Foot traffic appeared in 4/5 conversations, in 3 different phrasings. Reputation appeared in 5/5 — uniformly described as 'reviews.'"

### Section 2: Goals that didn't appear (or rarely)

If a goal in our catalog never came up, ask: is it a real goal owners have but rarely articulate, or is it our marketing-jargon framing of something they say differently?

### Section 3: Goals or pain points that appeared but aren't in our catalog

This is the most valuable section. New material.

### Section 4: Vendor war stories (verbatim quotes)

Use these as marketing copy later. They prove the moat is real.

### Section 5: Owner language we should adopt

Words and phrasings that should replace our current copy.

Send synthesis to me. We update the catalog (or don't) before the goal layer build.

---

## Time budget per conversation

- 5 min: warm-up, set expectations
- 5 min: Q1 (last week walkthrough)
- 4 min: Q2 (signal vs noise)
- 3 min: Q3 (one task to hand off)
- 5 min: Q4 (vendor experience)
- 5 min: Q5 (success in 12 months)
- 3 min: open question — "anything else you wish I'd asked?"

Most conversations will run long. That's fine. Don't end early to stay on schedule.

---

## A note on bias

You'll be tempted to interpret answers favorably toward the spec. Resist. The whole point of these conversations is finding where the spec is wrong. **A conversation that confirms everything is suspicious. A conversation that surprises you is valuable.**

If 5 conversations all perfectly confirm the catalog, either you got lucky or you led the witness. Re-read your notes for leading questions.

If 1 of 5 surprises you in some way — that's the conversation to dwell on.
